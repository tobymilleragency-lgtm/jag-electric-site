**H1:** Electrical Panel Upgrades — Pittsburg, KS Area

**Lead paragraph:**
Your electrical panel controls every circuit in your home or building. It's the one piece of equipment that everything else depends on. When it's working right, you never think about it. ---

**Why Panel Upgrades Matter**

The electrical panels in older homes were designed for a fraction of the load a modern household puts on them. Panels sized at 60 or 100 amps made sense when a home ran a refrigerator, a few lights, and a television. Today that same panel is being asked to handle central air, an electric range, a dishwasher, two home office setups, a game system, and possibly a hot tub or EV charger. Something has to give — and it usually gives at the worst time.

Beyond load capacity, older panels can be a safety issue. Fuse boxes and some older breaker panels are known fire risks. Panels with original breakers from the 1970s or 1980s may no longer trip reliably when they should. If your panel is that age, it's worth having it looked at — not because something is definitely wrong, but because you should know what you have.

---

**Signs You May Need a Panel Upgrade**

Pay attention to any of these:

- Breakers that trip frequently, especially under normal household use
- A fuse box still in place instead of a circuit breaker panel
- Lights that flicker or dim when large appliances kick on
- Circuits that feel underpowered — a hair dryer trips a breaker, for example
- You're running extension cords or power strips throughout the house to make up for a lack of outlets
- You're adding a major load: workshop, hot tub, EV charger, home addition, or HVAC upgrade
- You're buying an older home and the inspector flagged the panel
- Your homeowner's insurance is asking about the panel

Any one of these is a reason to call. Taken together, they're a clear sign.

---

**What a Panel Upgrade Involves**

The most common upgrade is a service entrance replacement — pulling the old panel, installing a new one with the right amperage for your needs, and making sure all the circuits are properly wired and labeled. A standard residential upgrade takes the panel from 100A to 200A service, which is enough for most modern households.

In some cases, we install a sub-panel to handle a detached garage, workshop, or separate structure on the property. Sub-panels run from your main panel and give you dedicated capacity for that space without running individual circuits back to the main.

Work also includes:
- Full panel replacement with new breakers
- Service upgrades (60A to 100A, 100A to 200A, or larger for commercial)
- Sub-panel installation for garages, outbuildings, and large additions
- Circuit additions at the time of panel replacement
- Labeling and organizing existing circuits
- Coordinating with the utility for disconnect/reconnect when required

We'll tell you up front what's involved for your specific situation. No pressure and no upsell — just a straight answer on what you need and what it costs.

We work with homeowners and general contractors to size and place panels correctly for the project scope, and we handle the rough-in and finish electrical as part of the build.

For remodels where walls are open, it's also a natural time to evaluate the existing panel. If you're already pulling permits and opening up the structure, adding a panel upgrade to the scope can save you from coming back later.

### SERVICE PAGE: Rewiring (/services/rewiring)

**H1:** Home Rewiring Services — Southeast Kansas

**Lead paragraph:**
Older homes carry a lot of character. They also carry a lot of wiring that was installed decades before anyone thought about the electrical loads modern life demands. Knob-and-tube wiring from the early 1900s. Aluminum wiring from the 1960s and 70s. Circuits that run through walls, attics, and crawl spaces with no roadmap and no documentation. If you're in an older home and you've never had the wiring evaluated, you may not know what's actually in those walls.

JAG Electric provides residential rewiring services for older homes, full remodels, and additions throughout the Pittsburg and Galena area. We'll walk you through what's there, what the risks are, and what it takes to bring it up to safe, current-code standards.

---

**Why Wiring Gets Replaced**

Rewiring is one of those projects homeowners put off because it sounds expensive and disruptive. Sometimes it is. But there are situations where it's the right call — and knowing the difference is what the assessment is for.

The most common reasons homes get rewired:

**Age of the home.** Homes built before 1970 often have wiring that's approaching or past its serviceable life. Insulation on older wire becomes brittle and cracks. Connections loosen. The risk of arc faults — where electricity jumps outside its intended path and ignites nearby material — increases with age.

**Knob-and-tube wiring.** This is the cloth-wrapped, ceramic-insulator style of wiring found in homes built before the 1940s. It's not inherently dangerous if it's undisturbed and in original condition, but it doesn't allow for grounded circuits, can't support modern electrical loads safely, and most insurers will flag it or refuse to cover homes with it active.

**Aluminum wiring.** Used widely in the late 1960s and into the 1970s as a copper substitute during metal shortages, aluminum wiring expands and contracts with temperature changes and can loosen connections over time. It's a known fire risk and typically requires remediation — either replacing the wiring or installing approved aluminum-to-copper splices at every connection and device.

**Remodel or addition.** If you're opening walls for a kitchen, bathroom, or room addition, it's the right time to address any wiring in that section of the house. You won't have the same access later, and the incremental cost of updating the wiring while walls are already open is far less than tearing into finished walls afterward.

**Insurance requirements.** Some insurers require rewiring as a condition of coverage or renewal, particularly in older homes. If your insurer has flagged your wiring, we can assess what's involved.

---

**What Rewiring Typically Involves**

Full rewiring means pulling the old wiring throughout the home and running new copper wiring to all outlets, switches, fixtures, and appliances. For most homes, this means accessing the walls from attic and basement where possible to minimize drywall cuts, and making targeted cuts where needed.

Scope and timeline depend on:
- Size of the home
- Accessibility of walls, attic, and crawl space
- Whether walls are already open (remodel) or finished
- Age and type of existing wiring
- Number of circuits and devices

We'll do a thorough walkthrough before we give you a number. The estimate will cover what access we need, how we protect your home during the work, and what the end result looks like. No vague ballparks — a real scope.

---

**Partial Rewiring and Targeted Work**

Not every job is a full house rewire. Sometimes it's a specific zone — a kitchen that needs new circuits, a basement being finished, a room addition, or a detached garage. We handle partial rewiring and targeted circuit work as well.

If you're not sure whether you need a full rewire or something more targeted, that's exactly what the estimate conversation is for.

---

**What to Expect After Rewiring**

New wiring brings every outlet, switch, and fixture up to current code — properly grounded, properly sized, properly connected. You'll have circuits that can handle the loads you're actually running. Your insurance situation typically improves. And you won't be dealing with mystery tripping breakers or flickering lights caused by aging wire and loose connections.

**H1:** Outlets, Switches & Circuit Work — Southeast Kansas

**Lead paragraph:**
An outlet that stopped working. A switch that sparks when you flip it. A bathroom with no GFCI protection in a house built 30 years ago. ---

**What We Handle**

**Outlet Repair and Replacement**
Standard duplex outlets wear out over time. The internal contacts get loose, the ground blade slot cracks, and eventually the outlet stops holding a plug or stops delivering power. Replacement is straightforward — and it's the kind of thing that should be done rather than ignored, particularly if the outlet is warm to the touch or shows signs of arcing.

**GFCI Outlets**
Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter outlets are required by electrical code near water sources — kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoor locations, and unfinished basement areas. If your home was built or last updated before these codes became standard, you may have none or inadequate coverage. GFCI installation is a quick, cost-effective upgrade that protects against shock in wet areas.

If a GFCI outlet trips frequently, it's doing its job — but it might also indicate a wiring problem upstream that needs to be diagnosed.

**Switch Replacement**
Single-pole switches, three-way switches, and four-way switches all fail eventually. A switch that doesn't click cleanly, requires excessive force, or fails to make a reliable connection is a replacement job. We also install dimmer switches, timer switches, and smart-switch replacements.

**Adding New Outlets**
A lot of older homes don't have enough outlets in the right places. Running a new outlet to a garage, adding outlets to a kitchen that only has two circuits, or putting outlets on exterior walls that have none — these are standard circuit addition jobs. We run new wiring from the panel, install the outlet, and leave you with a clean, properly installed addition.

**USB and Smart Outlets**
Combination USB/outlet receptacles for charging, smart outlets that can be controlled remotely, and high-amperage outlets for appliances — we install these as replacements or as part of outlet addition work.

**Computer Cable and Data Line Installation**
JAG Electric handles computer cable and data line installation as part of our outlet work — running ethernet and data lines through walls and to wall plates for clean, professional structured wiring.

---

**When Outlets and Switches Point to Bigger Problems**

Sometimes a dead outlet is just a dead outlet. Sometimes it's the symptom of something larger — a tripped GFCI upstream that hasn't been reset, a loose connection in the wiring, a circuit that's overloaded, or an issue at the panel. We diagnose before we replace. If the outlet died because of an underlying wiring problem, you'll know before we're done.

Same with switches. A switch that sparks on contact, a switch that feels warm, or a switch that doesn't reliably complete the circuit can indicate a loose wire connection or a wiring problem that needs attention beyond the switch itself.

---

Current electrical code requires GFCI protection in:
- Bathrooms
- Kitchens (within 6 feet of a sink)

- Outdoors
- Unfinished basements

If your home doesn't have GFCI outlets in these locations, it may be out of compliance with current code. More practically — it's unprotected. GFCI upgrades are fast and inexpensive. Call us if you're not sure where you stand.

**FAQ: Outlets & Switches**

Q: My outlet stopped working but the breaker didn't trip. What happened?
A: The most common cause is a tripped GFCI outlet upstream — there's a GFCI elsewhere on the same circuit, and when it tripped, it cut power to everything downstream. Check your bathrooms and kitchen for GFCI outlets with a tripped reset button. If that's not it, call us.

Q: Can I replace an outlet myself?
A: If you know what you're doing and the wiring is straightforward, yes. But if you're not confident, it's not worth the risk. Call us — outlet replacement is not an expensive job.

Q: How many outlets can be on one circuit?
A: The code doesn't set a hard limit on outlets per circuit, but overcrowded circuits that draw too much load cause breaker trips. If you're constantly tripping a breaker, the circuit may need to be split or a new circuit added.

Q: Do I need GFCI outlets if my bathroom already has a GFCI breaker?
A: A GFCI breaker on the panel protects the whole circuit, which can substitute for individual GFCI outlets. But older homes often have neither. If you're unsure, call us and we'll look.
